about
 I am an evolutionary biologist interested in genome evolution and genetic systems. I use theory, experiments, and statistical analysis of DNA sequences to understand the processes that shape the evolution of genomes and reproductive systems. I received my Ph.D. from the University of Toronto, where I worked with Spencer C.H. Barrett and Stephen I. Wright on the evolution and genetics of sex chromosomes in plants. Currently, I am a postdoctoral research fellow in Jeffery Ross-Ibarra's lab at the University of California, where I am investigating the fitness effects of new mutations and the mechanisms that constrain adaptive molecular evolution in the Maize genome.

research
 I am particularly fascinated by the evolutionary biology of reproductive and mating systems, and I am motivated by questions such as: Why do some species reproduce by mating with other individuals (outcrossing), while others mate with themselves (self-fertilizing), while others still reproduce without sex at all? How do differences in the extent of such "genetic mixing" (recombination and segregation) within the genome, or on certain chromosomes, affect the outcome of natural selection and genetic drift? I am also interested more generally in mutations: what determines how often they occur, their effects on fitness, and the factors that limit the ability of selection to act on them.
