about
 I am an evolutionary biologist interested in genome evolution and genetic systems. I use theory, experiments, and statistical analysis of DNA sequences to make inferences about the mechanisms that contribute to evolutionary change. I received my Ph.D. from the University of Toronto, where I worked with Spencer C.H. Barrett and Stephen I. Wright on the evolution and genetics of sex chromosomes in plants. Currently, I am a postdoctoral research fellow in Jeffery Ross-Ibarra's lab at the University of California, where I am investigating the fitness effects of new mutations and the mechanisms that constrain adaptive molecular evolution in the Maize genome.

research
 I am particularly fascinated by the evolutionary aspects of reproductive systems. I am motivated by questions such as: why do some species reproduce by mating with other individuals (outcrossing), while others mate with themselves (self-fertilizing), and others reproduce without sex at all? How do differences in the extent of such "genetic mixing" (i.e., recombination and segregation) within the genome, or on certain chromosomes, affect the outcome of natural selection and genetic drift? I am also interested more generally in mutations: what determines how often they occur, their effects on fitness (and why this can be different for males and females), and the factors that limit the ability of natural selection to act on them. Because of the incredible amount of genetic system diversity in plants, they are an excellent group in which to ask these questions, and my research has primarily used flowering plant species as models to test ideas about how evolution works.
